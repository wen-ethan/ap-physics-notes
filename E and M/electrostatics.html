Unit 1 - Electrostatics

Charging Objects via Induction and Conduction

Conductors

  • Inside, $E = 0$.
  • Outside, $E$ behaves like a point charge.

Insulators

  • Inside, $E \neq 0$, use charge density to find $Q_{enc}$:
    • Uniform:
      • $Q_{enc} = \rho V$
      • $Q_{enc}=Q_{total} (\frac{r^3}{R^3})$
    • Non-Uniform:
      • $Q_{enc}= \int \rho \,dv$
  • Outside, $E$ behaves like a point charge.

Grounding

Coulomb's Law for Discrete Point Charges

$F_{E} = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \, \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$

  • Magnitude of the electric field of a point charge can be obtained through Coulomb's Law: $|\vec{E}| = \frac{F_{E}}{q} = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \, \frac{Q}{r^2}$.
  • Used to find forces on charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ when they are a distance $r$ apart from one another.
  • Use Coulomb's Law to find the magnitude of the force, then find the direction of the force seperately.
    • Like charges repel one another, while opposite charges attract.

Electric Field Due to Some Source on a Test Charge

  • Test charges are always positive, unless otherwise stated.

$|\vec{E}| = \frac{F_{E}}{q} = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \, \frac{Q}{r^2}$

  • Calculate as magnitude, then use the electric field lines to find direction.
    • Positively-charged ions follow electric field lines.
    • Negatively-charged ions oppose electric field lines.

Continuous Line/Arc of Charge

$dE = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{dq}{r^2} \, \hat{r}$

When calculating magnitude, $dE = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{dq}{r^2} \,$.

Gauss's Law
